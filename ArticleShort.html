A Metaphor for Convergence

Elizabeth Short, MD

Museum of the American West Docent, Class of 2006

This beautifully woven Métis sash is a small symbol of the fusion of cultural traditions in the relationships of the French and Native Americans in the eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries. The French voyageurs became deeply intertwined with the Indian tribes of the Northeast, learning their languages, experiencing their cultures, and in many cases intermarrying. Métis is the designation for the descendants of such unions, who pride themselves on their origins and rich traditions. For them, the sash is a symbol of this heritage, since the French Canadian traders learned fingerweaving techniques from the Eastern Woodlands Indians and contributed their colorful imported wools. Both cultures wove and wore the sash; it was worn by Eastern Woodlands Indians as a sign of office in the nineteenth century and by French Canadians during the Lower Canada Rebellion of 1837. A variation of the sash became a popular trade item and was woven as a cottage industry in the village of L’Assomption, Quebec. This came to be called the Assumption sash.

Because I am a handweaver, this sash is especially meaningful to me as a beautiful object that combines a cultural symbol with a demon stration of notable skill and technique. For me, the deeper meaning of the sash is that it represents a laudable model for the arrival of a foreign culture in this country. That model is integration, intermarriage, and peaceful respect for one another’s skills and traditions, so that the intertwining of the two cultures produces a new and rich creation, like the sash of the Métis.
